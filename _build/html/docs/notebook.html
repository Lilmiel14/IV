Introduction#

Social media is a platform where students spend plenty of time. Students often use social media applications such as Instagram, Snapchat, Facebook etc. Social media has a positive as well as a negative impact on students and through this data-driven story we will dive deeper into these impacts.

We will make use of datasets to dive deep into the social media industry, exploring user statistics, and effects of social media. By analyzing these data, we aim to gain valuable insights into the evolving landscape of social media. Through visualizations of social media and screentime datasets, we identify user preferences and explore the factors driving the growth and innovation within the social media industry.

In this story we have a few perspectives about social media. First of all, social media increases the awareness of students. Because of social media, students gain access to a wealth of information and stay updated on important events and daily news. Social media provided real-time updates and a diverse range of perspectives, allowing students to be rapidly informed. Furthermore, social media has revolutionized communication. It connects people from across the globe and allows people to share information and experiences. Social media has given people and businesses a platform to engage in different communities and express themselves.

However, there is a dark side of social media. Students can be more exposed to fake news. It is easy for people to spread misinformation, which can lead to a rapid increase of false narratives. Due to the nature of social media, these false narratives can reach a wide audience before the information is verified. According to a research of scholars from Stanford (Spector, 2019), students don’t have enough ability to recognise fake news, so they will believe faster what is on the internet without being critical. Moreover, excessive use of social media can lead to problems such as addiction and health issues. The exposure of filtered content on social media platforms can set unrealistic expectations and can cause people to have a low self-esteem.

This story consists of a few components. The first chapter is the “Dataset en preprocessing”. In this chapter are the datasets described and how we preprocess them. Preprocess means where we found the dataset and how we processed the dataset into a visualization. The next chapter consists of the dataset visualizations with each a caption and title. At last, we will dive deeper into our perspectives with multiple arguments.

Dataset and preprocessing#

1 Average screentime#

The first dataset will show the average screen time of 45 countries. The dataset includes information such as time spent on devices, time spent on social media and the usage of the internet via mobiles, computers, etc. Worldwide, the average person has a screentime of 6 hours and 37 minutes. This dataset provides an opportunity to analyze the digital habits and trends across different countries. The initial dataset was obtained from kaggle.com in the form of a CSV file. To portray the average duration of device usage and social media engagement per country, the data underwent preprocessing. In order to appropriately preprocess the data, the time variable was converted into a decimal value. Both the variables ‘Time Spent on Devices’ and ‘Time Spent using Social Media’ are graphically represented in a bar chart.

2 Effects of social media#

The second dataset contains data about the usage of social media by students. Information about the students’ habits and preferences are provided, such as their preferred social media platform, the amount of time they spend on social media and their preferred communication methods. The dataset provides opportunities to analyze the impact of social media on students’ lives. The subsequent dataset, sourced from data.world, was provided as an XLSX file. Prior to analysis, the data was subjected to preprocessing to illustrate the occurrences of various types of cybercrime victims and the favored mode of communication. Both aspects are presented individually through two distinct donut charts. Since participants were allowed to select multiple options, the occurrences of each option were aggregated. Using the same dataset employed for the donut charts, we further processed the data to showcase the daily levels of physical activity and social interaction. These statistics are visually depicted using two pie charts.

3 Factors contributing to internet addiction.#

The third dataset provides data about factors contributing to internet addiction across a diverse age group. Among the participants, 6.7% were classified as addictive internet users. The main contributing factors were found to be a high use of discussion forums, a lack of self care and engaging in online video gaming. By analyzing the different factors that influence internet addiction across different age groups, it’s possible to understand the underlying problem. The third dataset was discovered on figshare.com in the form of a SAV file. For appropriate preprocessing, the columns in the file were renamed to enhance the overall presentation, for instance, changing ‘DASS21_depression’ to ‘depression’. Subsequently, a box plot was generated to visualize the anxiety, depression, and stress levels of individuals who engage in social media usage.

4 Fake news#

Social media has become the biggest platform for sharing different types of content, including news articles. The fourth dataset provides information about the authenticity of articles from different websites. The dataset contains variables such as author, publish date and label (real or fake). The dataset was found on kaggle.com in the form of a CSV file. Before creating the plot, the rows where the language was missing were removed. Furthermore, the values in the column “hasImage” were changed from binary to “Yes” and “No” for easier readability. With this data, a parallel categories plot was created, with the line color based on the “Real” and “Fake” label.

5 Social media privacy awareness#

The fifth dataset provides data about a survey conducted on 127 students at Institut Teknologi Sepuluh Nopember (ITS) Surabaya about their social media awareness. Variables such as age and sex are present in the dataset, as well as questions about the students’ social media usage, their personal information and privacy permissions. The dataset was found on zenodo.org in the form of an XLSX file. The language of the dataset was Indonesian, so the language was converted to English. Two new data frames were created from this dataset. The first one being questions that could be answered with “Yes” and “No” and the second being the degree of concern regarding social media. Both of the data frames were plotted using the seaborn library.

The perspectives will be analyzed and supported by arguments and the visualizations. We have a total of six visualizations. The first two visualizations will support the bright side of social media and the last four visualizations will support the dark side of social media.

df = pd.read_csv('screen.csv')

def time_to_decimal(time_str):
    hours, minutes = map(int, time_str.split(':'))
    decimal_hours = hours + minutes / 60
    return decimal_hours

df['Total Time Spent on Devices'] = df['Total Time Spent on Devices'].apply(time_to_decimal)
df['Time Spent using Social Media'] = df['Time Spent using Social Media'].apply(time_to_decimal)

countries = ['Netherlands', 'Worldwide', 'Brazil', 'Russia', 'USA', 'Indonesia', 'China', 'Australia', 'South Africa', 'UK', 'France', 'Germany', 'Saudi Arabia', 'India', 'Japan']

Figure 3: The figure presented above provides a visual about the relationship between the DASS (Depression, Anxiety and Stress Scale) and social network usage. The DASS scale is a psychological analysis tool to measure the severity of these three mental health conditions. It provides a quantitative measure for individuals who suffer from these conditions. Social network usage refers to the frequency to which the participants use online platforms to communicate. The network usage is ordered in an ordinal scale, ranging from the lowest level (‘Never’) to the highest level (‘Very often’). Information about the DASS is displayed in five different boxplots: one boxplot for each level.

A big problem with social media is that on the platforms the users will compare their lives to others, which can lead to low self-esteem, depression and anxiety. By creating such an environment, where individuals are constantly exposed to other people’s lives, the social media users can develop feelings of dissatisfaction with their own lives. As seen in the ‘Very often’ boxplot, all mental health conditions have a relatively max score. So the people who spend a lot of time using social media experience more mental health problems.

Figure 5: This visualization consists of a parallel categories diagram about the classification of fake news. The diagram contains four different variables: ‘Language’, ‘Type’, ‘Has image’, ‘Real of Fake’. The ‘Language’ variable represents the language of the articles, with the categories ‘English’, ‘German’, ‘French’, ‘Spanish’, and ‘Ignore’. The ‘Type’ variable represents the type of the articles, with the categories ‘bs’, ‘bias’, ‘conspiracy’, ‘hate’, ‘satire’, ‘state’, ‘junksci’, and ‘fake’. The ‘Has image’ variable represents whether the article has an image, with the categories ‘yes’ and ‘no’. The ‘Real or Fake’ variable represents the classification of the articles as either ‘real’ or ‘fake’.
